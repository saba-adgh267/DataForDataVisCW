The Green Divide: Tree Coverage and Deprivation in England's Neighbourhoods

When we think about inequality in England, we often focus on income, housing or education. But there’s another form of disadvantage that grows quietly in the background, or in some places, doesn’t grow at all. Trees.

Some neighbourhoods are shaped by leafy streets and shaded paths, while others feel bare and exposed. This isn’t just a visual difference. Trees help clean the air, cool down busy streets and create spaces that feel calmer and healthier. Yet these benefits aren’t shared evenly. Greener areas often coincide with wealth, while more deprived communities tend to have far fewer trees.

When you place this pattern alongside differences in pollution, density and health, the imbalance becomes even clearer. The places that could benefit most from tree cover often have the least of it.

To explore this divide, we examine three questions: whether wealth and tree coverage are actually connected, what this means for health and environmental quality and whether disadvantages compound when they occur together. Through regional-level data covering England's neighbourhoods, the patterns reveal themselves—and by the end, what they might mean for environmental justice becomes clearer.

What This Means

So what have we actually found here? Looking back across all three visualizations, the pattern is uncomfortable but clear: England's trees aren't distributed fairly, and this seems to have real consequences for the people living in these areas.

Let's start with what we know for certain. Wealthier regions have more trees—that relationship is undeniable. And those trees? They're strongly linked to better health. So right away, we're seeing that the communities already struggling economically also have less access to something that appears to benefit health. Now, health is complicated, and countless factors play into it beyond what we've looked at here. But a correlation this strong is hard to dismiss.

Then there's the pollution puzzle. You might expect that more trees would mean cleaner air, but that's where population density throws everything off. Take London—it's got decent tree coverage and actually shows the best health outcomes, yet it also has the worst pollution. Why? Because when you pack over 10,000 people into every square kilometer, the sheer volume of traffic and industry creates pollution that trees simply can't keep up with. Trees help, but they're not miracle workers.

But here's what really matters. When we look at places where low wealth and low tree coverage happen together—the North East, Yorkshire, the North West—we're not just seeing two problems sitting side by side. The health outcomes in these regions are worse than you'd expect from either disadvantage alone. It's as if the problems amplify each other. Meanwhile, the South East sits comfortably in the opposite corner with wealth, trees, and good health all aligned.

The data shows us something worth paying attention to. What comes next is a question of priorities and action.
